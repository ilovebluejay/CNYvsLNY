CNY vs LNY - a brief introduction

Intro:

CNY, or Chineese new year and LNY, or Lunar New Year are two very different things, and today i will tell you why.

CNY Info

CNY.

Chinese New Year, also known as the Spring Festival (春节, Chūnjié), is the most important traditional holiday in China and among Chinese communities worldwide. It's a time for family reunions, feasting, honoring ancestors, and celebrating new beginnings. Festivities can last up to 15 days, culminating in the Lantern Festival.

How is the Date Determined?
Chinese New Year is based on the Chinese lunisolar calendar, which combines elements of both the moon phases and the solar year.
Lunar Month Start:
The date always falls on the second new moon after the Winter Solstice, which typically happens between January 21 and February 20.
Solar Calendar Influence:
Since the lunar calendar doesn't align perfectly with the solar year (the one our Gregorian calendar uses), the date shifts each year.
Key Factors:
New Moon: CNY always begins on a new moon. Lunisolar Correction: The Chinese calendar occasionally adds a "leap month" (like a leap year) to align better with the solar calendar.

LNY, and their differences.

1. Broader Scope of Lunar New Year (LNY)
LNY encompasses various cultures across East and Southeast Asia, while CNY specifically refers to China's celebration. LNY is celebrated in: China: (Chinese New Year / 春节) Vietnam: (Tết Nguyên Đán) South Korea: (Seollal) Mongolia: (Tsagaan Sar) Singapore, Malaysia, and other Chinese communities.

2. Differences in Zodiac Animals: Although many cultures use the Chinese Zodiac, some have key variations:
Vietnam (Tết): Replaces the Rabbit with the Cat and Ox with Buffalo. Mongolia (Tsagaan Sar): Uses Mongolian animal names but follows the same 12-year cycle.
3. Regional Differences in Dates. LNY typically starts on the same new moon (between January 21 and February 20), but celebrations can vary:
Vietnam (Tết): Sometimes begins a day earlier or later depending on lunar calendar calculations. South Korea (Seollal): Often celebrated for three days, including the day before and after the new moon.
4. Unique Cultural Traditions:
China (CNY): Red envelopes, dragon/lion dances, and 15-day celebrations ending with the Lantern Festival. Vietnam (Tết): Ancestral altar offerings, eating bánh chưng (sticky rice cakes), and kumquat tree decorations. South Korea (Seollal): Bowing to elders (sebae) for blessings and eating tteokguk (rice cake soup). Mongolia (Tsagaan Sar): Dairy-based feasts and wearing traditional deel robes.
5. Food Specialties: China: Dumplings (jiǎozi), fish (symbolizing prosperity), and rice cakes. Vietnam: Bánh chưng (square sticky rice cakes) and pickled vegetables. Korea: Tteokguk (rice cake soup) and galbijjim (braised beef ribs). Mongolia: Boiled lamb, milk-based foods, and steamed dumplings (buuz).
